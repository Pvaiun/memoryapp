Memory · Calendar view · Round 2

The calendar is the page

Building on the open-grid direction: no persistent task list. The month fills the screen, days speak in keywords, and detail only appears when you expand a day. Three mechanisms, one per mock-up — a fisheye lens on the current week, days sized by what they hold, and a visual grammar that separates recurring rhythms from one-off moments. The data model already knows the difference (item.cadence vs. eventAt), so the "smart parsing" costs one filter.

The whole month stays on screen; the lens does the prioritizing. The selected week gets ~5× the height of a distant week and is the only place that renders words. Tap any other week (or scroll) and the lens glides there — you never lose the month's shape to see the week's content.

  • 1Three zoom levels, three vocabularies. Far weeks: number + density bar. The adjacent week: number + a one-word whisper ("Rent", "Anna"). The focus week: keyword chips, time hints, and multi-day spans as a continuous band — Sarah & Deidra reads as one visit, not five dots.
  • 2Rhythms don't inflate density. Daily/weekly cadence items would light up every bar, so they're excluded from the bars and appear only as faint ticks inside the focus week. The bars answer "which days are unusual?"
  • 3Detail is one tap deeper. Today shows a "3 items" pill; tapping any focus-week day expands it (see Concept C's panel) rather than keeping a permanent agenda below.

The grid rows in .cal-grid become per-week rows with a height class driven by distance from the selected week; byDay in CalendarView.tsx already holds per-day entries for chips and counts. A CSS height transition makes the lens glide.

The boldest option: the grid itself becomes the chart. Each week is a bar; each day's width is proportional to its load (rhythms excluded, so the proportions mean something). Empty days compress to labeled slivers, busy days earn enough room to speak in words. Week height scales too — the week of the visit is physically the biggest thing on screen.

  • 1You read the month like a skyline. Where the space is, the life is. No counting dots — the visit week and the end-of-month cluster are visible from arm's length.
  • 2Partial weeks stop pretending. July 1–4 renders as a four-day bar; there are no ghost cells from June or August at all.
  • 3The honest tradeoff: weekday columns no longer align vertically, so each day carries its own "T 23" label. You trade "all Mondays in a column" for "busy days are big." Worth prototyping; feels great or infuriating, nothing in between.

Each week becomes a flex row with flex: 1 + moments.length per day — about 30 lines in CalendarView.tsx. Cap the ratio (e.g. max 4:1) so quiet days stay tappable.

Every mark answers "what kind of time is this?" Recurring rhythms (daily dishwasher, every-other-day garden) recede into faint ticks at the cell floor — present, never loud. Weekly recurrences render as a hollow ring that visibly repeats down the Friday column. One-off moments get the only words on the grid: an auto-keyword clipped from the title. Multi-day events run as a single band.

  • 1The month becomes scannable prose. "Dentist… Payday… Sarah & Deidra… Market… Rent" is the month, readable top to bottom, because rhythms no longer camouflage the moments.
  • 2Detail lives behind the tap. Selecting a day unfolds an inline panel under its own week row — full titles, cadence, checkboxes — labeled rhythm or moment. Close it and the calendar is all there is.
  • 3The parse is one line. item.cadence → tick (or ring if weekly); eventAt / deadline → keyword chip; eventEnd spanning days → band. The keyword is the title's first two significant words.

The calendar API already returns kind per entry and full items with cadence/eventAt/eventEnd; the accordion is a conditional row inserted after the selected week in CalendarView.tsx.

Recommendation

C is the language, A is the camera

Concept C's grammar should exist in any version — ticks for rhythms, keywords for moments, bands for spans, and the tap-to-expand day panel. It's what makes a words-on-the-grid calendar readable instead of noisy, and the data model already supports it.

Layer Concept A on top: the fisheye simply decides how much of that grammar each week gets to speak — bars far away, whispers nearby, full chips under the lens. Together they're one coherent page: a full month you never leave, that always spends its pixels where your attention is.

B is the wildcard. It's the most striking and the most opinionated — it gives up aligned weekday columns. I'd build A + C first, then prototype B's width-weighting inside the fisheye's focus week only, where the alignment loss costs least.
